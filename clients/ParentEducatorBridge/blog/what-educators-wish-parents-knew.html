What Parents Wish Educators Knew Before the IEP Meeting

October 15, 2024 8 min read

After thirty-five years working in special education—first as a parent advocate, then as a special education teacher, and now as someone who trains both parents and educators—I've sat at hundreds of IEP tables. I've experienced the tension, the misunderstandings, the moments when what could have been a productive partnership dissolved into defensiveness and frustration.

I've been the parent whose hands shook while signing an IEP, terrified I was missing something that would matter later. I've been the teacher who stayed up past midnight preparing data, hoping parents would see how much I cared. I've witnessed brilliant collaboration and devastating breakdowns.

And through it all, I've learned something crucial: Most conflicts in IEP meetings aren't about bad intentions. They're about not understanding each other's reality.

This article isn't about pointing fingers or assigning blame. It's about building bridges. Because when parents and educators truly understand each other, magic happens. Children get what they need. Teams work together instead of against each other. And everyone leaves the table feeling heard, respected, and hopeful.

So here's what I wish every educator knew before walking into an IEP meeting—straight from the parent perspective.

What Parents Wish You Knew

1. We're Terrified

When parents walk into that conference room, their hearts are racing. This isn't just another meeting for us. This is our child. Our future. Our everything.

We're thinking about whether our child will be able to live independently someday, hold a job, have friends, be safe. The stakes feel impossibly high because they are.

If we seem defensive, it's often because we've been burned before. Maybe a previous school told us our concerns weren't valid. Maybe we fought for years to get services only to be told the problem was our parenting. Maybe we've been dismissed, patronized, or made to feel like we're overreacting.

Our intensity doesn't come from disrespect for you. It comes from love for our child and fear that if we don't advocate hard enough, they won't get what they need.

What This Looks Like

The parent who interrupts: They're not trying to be rude. They're terrified they'll forget to say something important.

The parent who brings a advocate: They're not preparing for battle. They're trying to feel less alone and overwhelmed.

The parent who cries: This is vulnerability, not manipulation. They're showing you how much this matters.

2. We Don't Understand Your World

Educational jargon is a foreign language to most parents. LRE, FAPE, PLAAFP, SDI, ESY, BIP, FBA—these acronyms might as well be alphabet soup.

We don't know what's legally required versus what's a nice-to-have. We don't understand the difference between an accommodation and a modification. We're not sure what's reasonable to ask for and what's impossible.

And here's what makes it worse: We've often been given terrible advice.

We've joined Facebook groups where well-meaning parents tell horror stories and share adversarial strategies. We've read blog posts that paint schools as the enemy. We've been told never to trust what educators say, to record everything, to assume bad faith.

So when you use technical terms or assume we understand the process, we feel lost. And when we feel lost, we get scared. And when we get scared, we get defensive.

"The parents who seem the most difficult are often the most confused. They're not fighting you. They're fighting their own overwhelm."

3. We've Been Told to "Fight"

There's an entire cottage industry of parent advocates and attorneys who teach parents that schools are adversaries. That every IEP meeting is a battle. That you should never sign anything without a lawyer review. That educators are trying to do the bare minimum.

Some parents show up braced for a fight that doesn't need to happen. They've been told to be suspicious of everything you say. To assume you're holding back services. To bring a tape recorder and a lawyer.

This isn't because they think you're a bad person. It's because they've been told this is the only way to protect their child.

When you encounter a parent in "battle mode," remember: Someone taught them that this was necessary. Your job is to show them it's not.

4. We Know Things You Don't

We know our child better than anyone on earth. We've watched them since birth. We know their triggers, their joys, their fears, their patterns. We know what helps them calm down, what makes them anxious, what they're capable of when they feel safe.

We see behaviors at home that you don't see at school. We notice patterns you might miss. We have insights that could be invaluable to their education.

But sometimes our expertise is dismissed as "just a parent's perspective."

When you ask us genuine questions about our child and truly listen to the answers, you're not just being polite. You're accessing crucial information that could change your approach and improve outcomes.

Our observations aren't interference. They're collaboration.

5. We're Exhausted

This isn't a 9-to-5 job for us. This is 24/7, 365 days a year.

We're coordinating therapies—speech on Tuesdays, occupational therapy on Thursdays, physical therapy twice a week, plus the new social skills group that just opened up. We're fighting with insurance companies who deny coverage. We're researching interventions at 2 AM because our child is struggling and we're desperate to help.

We're managing medications, tracking behaviors, implementing home programs, attending endless appointments, filling out forms, making phone calls, sending emails.

And then we have to find the emotional and mental energy for an IEP meeting.

If we seem scattered or overwhelmed, it's because we are. If we don't have time to read a 40-page draft IEP the night before the meeting, it's not because we don't care. It's because we're running on empty.

6. We Need Hope

Yes, we need to know about challenges. Yes, we need accurate information about our child's struggles. But we also desperately need to hear about possibilities.

Don't just tell us the problems. Tell us what's possible.

Show us the progress, even if it's small. Tell us about the moment our child helped a classmate or showed kindness or mastered a skill they've been working on for months. Help us see a future that doesn't terrify us.

Every parent of a child with special needs has moments of fear about the future. Will my child be okay? Will they have friends? Will they be able to support themselves? Will someone take care of them when I'm gone?

When you share genuine positives and celebrate small wins, you're not just being nice. You're giving us the hope we need to keep going.

7. We Want to Be Partners

Despite what the Facebook groups might suggest, most parents aren't looking for a fight. We're looking for allies. We want to work together. We need to feel like we're on the same team.

We need to feel heard. Not just listened to, but truly heard. We need to know that our concerns matter and that you value our input.

We need to be included as equals at the table. Not talked down to. Not patronized. Not made to feel like we're just "the parents" while the "experts" make the real decisions.

When you treat us as partners—as equal members of the team who bring unique and valuable expertise—everything changes.

How Educators Can Help

Now that you understand what parents are experiencing, here's how you can make IEP meetings better for everyone.

Before the Meeting

  • Send the draft IEP in advance (this is required by law, but often happens late or not at all). Give parents actual time to review it—not the night before.
  • Share data ahead of time so parents can process it without the pressure of the meeting.
  • Ask what the parents want to discuss. A simple email saying "What are your priorities for this IEP meeting?" can help everyone prepare.
  • Explain who will be there and why. A room full of strangers is intimidating. A brief introduction email helps.

During the Meeting

  • Start with genuine positives. Not generic "she's a pleasure to have in class" statements, but specific examples of growth, strengths, or moments of success.
  • Use plain language, not jargon. If you must use an acronym, explain it every time. Don't assume parents remember from last year.
  • Ask parents what they're seeing at home. And then actually listen to the answer. Take notes. Show that their insights matter.
  • Listen more than you talk. Resist the urge to fill silence. Give parents space to process and respond.
  • Don't gang up. When seven professionals sit on one side of the table and two parents sit on the other, it feels adversarial even if you don't mean it that way. Space out the seating.

When Delivering Hard News

  • Lead with compassion. "This is hard to hear" or "I know this isn't what you were hoping for" acknowledges the emotional weight.
  • Have data to support your concerns. Vague statements like "he's not making progress" aren't helpful. Specific data is.
  • Offer solutions, not just problems. Don't just say "reading is a struggle." Say "reading is a struggle, and here's what we're planning to do about it."
  • Give parents time to process. Don't expect immediate agreement when you've just shared difficult information.

When Parents Are Upset

  • Don't get defensive. Their upset isn't about you personally (even if it feels that way).
  • Acknowledge their feelings. "I can see this is really upsetting" or "I understand your frustration" can defuse tension.
  • Take a break if needed. "Would it help to take a 10-minute break?" is a perfectly reasonable thing to offer.
  • Remember—this is their child. Your professional boundaries are important, but this is their life.

Building the Relationship

  • Communicate regularly, not just at meetings. A quick email sharing a success or asking how things are going at home builds trust.
  • Share successes, not just problems. Parents need to hear when things are going well.
  • Be accessible and responsive. Returning emails promptly shows you care.
  • Follow through on commitments. If you say you'll try something or send something, do it. Trust is built on reliability.

The Educators' Reality (Let's Acknowledge This Too)

Here's what I also want parents to understand, because I've been on your side of the table too:

You're also constrained by resources. You would love to give every child one-on-one support, but you have 20+ other students and limited staff.

You're following policies you didn't create. You don't make the district rules, the state mandates, or the federal laws. You're working within a system, not dictating it.

You're doing your best with what you have. You chose this profession because you care about kids. You stay late, spend your own money, and lose sleep over your students.

You're human. You make mistakes. You get tired. You have bad days. You're juggling impossible demands.

Parents need to understand this too. And when they do—when both sides see each other's humanity and constraints—real partnership becomes possible.

Meeting in the Middle

Here's the truth that both sides need to remember:

Both sides want the child to succeed. You're not enemies. You're partners with the same goal.

Both sides have constraints. Parents are dealing with limited time, energy, and resources. Educators are dealing with limited staff, budget, and support.

Both sides have valuable knowledge. Parents know their child. Educators know pedagogy, child development, and effective interventions.

Partnership serves the child best. When parents and educators work together—really work together—children get better outcomes, faster progress, and more support.

"The best IEP meetings I've ever been part of weren't the ones where everyone agreed on everything. They were the ones where everyone felt heard, respected, and valued. Where the conversation was honest and the commitment was real."

So to my fellow educators: The next time you sit down for an IEP meeting and see a parent who seems defensive, demanding, or difficult, remember this article. Remember that they're terrified, exhausted, and desperately trying to do right by their child.

Lead with compassion. Speak in plain language. Listen more than you talk. Treat them as the experts on their child that they are.

And watch what happens when you do.

Because when we stop talking at each other and start talking with each other, everything changes.

That's when we build bridges. That's when we serve children well. That's when we all get to go home feeling like we made a difference.

And isn't that why we all showed up in the first place?
