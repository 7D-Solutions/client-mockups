The 5 Things I Wish Someone Had Told Me When My Child Was Diagnosed

November 15, 2024
12 min read
Dr. Rebecca Martinez
After 35 years as a school psychologist, I've sat across the table from thousands of parents at that first IEP meeting. I've seen the fear in their eyes, watched them try to process a diagnosis, and felt their desperation to do right by their child. I wish I could go back and tell them what I know now—not to scare them, but to empower them. Here are the five things I wish someone had told every parent when their child was diagnosed.

1. School Cannot Replace What You Teach at Home

Let me start with something that might be hard to hear: The most important teaching your child will ever receive doesn't happen in a classroom.

Here's the math that changed how I talk to parents: Your child spends approximately 1,080 hours per year in school (6 hours a day × 180 days). They spend more than 3,650 hours awake at home with you. That's more than triple the time. Yet I've watched countless parents put all their energy into fighting for school services while overlooking the enormous impact they have at home.

3,650+ hours per year your child learns at home vs. 1,080 at school

The foundational skills that make everything else possible—using the bathroom independently, asking for help, understanding "no," following simple directions—these aren't primarily school skills. They're home skills. And no IEP, no matter how well-written, can compensate for these skills not being built at home first.

I remember working with Marcus, a bright 7-year-old with autism. His parents were devastated by his diagnosis and immediately channeled all their energy into his IEP. They fought for every minute of services, every accommodation. But at home, they backed off. They felt guilty. They stopped asking him to do things for himself. They'd bring him his shoes, dress him, anticipate every need.

After a year of intensive school services with minimal progress, his mom finally asked me, "Why isn't he getting better?" The truth was hard to share: he wasn't getting enough practice at home. School was teaching him skills in isolation, but he wasn't using them in real life because his parents had stopped expecting him to.

This isn't about blame—it's about recognizing your power. Your consistency matters more than any program. When you teach your child to pull up their pants after using the bathroom, you're not just teaching toileting. You're teaching sequencing, motor planning, independence, and self-care. When you require them to say "help" instead of screaming, you're teaching communication, emotional regulation, and problem-solving.

I've watched families transform their children's progress by making simple changes at home: requiring eye contact before giving a snack, waiting for a verbal request instead of anticipating needs, creating consistent routines, and following through every single time.

Key Takeaway:

  • Don't outsource the basics. School can enhance, but you build the foundation.
  • Consistency beats intensity. Daily practice at home matters more than weekly therapy.
  • Your expectations shape their capabilities. When you expect less, you get less.
  • Real-life practice is irreplaceable. Skills learned in isolation don't automatically generalize.
  • You are not "just a parent." You are your child's first and most important teacher.

2. 1:1 Support Isn't Always the Answer

This one breaks my heart every time I see it happen. A parent fights tooth and nail for a full-time 1:1 aide, celebrates when they get it, and then watches their child become completely dependent on adult support while missing out on peer interaction.

The goal of special education is independence, not constant support. Yet I've seen 1:1 aides become the opposite of what parents hoped for.

Let me paint you a picture of what this looks like in real life: Emma is in third grade with a full-time aide. During group work, the other kids naturally start talking to each other, problem-solving, negotiating roles. Emma's aide immediately steps in, facilitating, prompting, doing the social interaction for her. At recess, Emma's aide shadows her to every activity. The other kids stop inviting her because the aide is always there. At lunch, Emma sits with her aide instead of her peers.

Emma's parents got exactly what they asked for—full-time support. But by fifth grade, Emma couldn't function without an adult present. She had no friends. She never learned to navigate conflict, make mistakes, or figure things out on her own. The aide had become a buffer between Emma and every learning opportunity.

Children with special needs don't just learn from adults. In fact, some of the most important learning happens from watching, imitating, and interacting with peers. They learn social rules by seeing other kids follow them. They learn resilience by watching how other kids handle disappointment. They learn problem-solving by working through conflicts with classmates.

When an aide is always present, children can become socially isolated. Other kids see them as different—not because of their disability, but because they always have an adult attached to them. Natural peer interactions don't happen when there's a grown-up hovering.

"The best support is the least amount of support needed for success."

Now, I'm not saying 1:1 aides are never appropriate. For some children, particularly those with significant behavioral or medical needs, they're essential for safety and access. But they should always come with a plan to fade the support. Always.

The questions to ask aren't "Can we get a 1:1?" but rather:

  • What specific skills does my child need to develop to be more independent?
  • What's the least restrictive support that would allow my child to access the curriculum?
  • What's the plan to systematically reduce this support?
  • How are we building peer relationships and natural supports?
  • What opportunities for independence are we creating?

I've seen much better outcomes with strategic support—an aide who checks in periodically, who provides support during challenging transitions, who teaches skills and then backs off so the child can practice. This kind of support builds independence instead of dependence.

Key Takeaway:

  • More support isn't always better support. Sometimes it's actually worse.
  • Peers are teachers too. Don't let adult support replace peer interaction.
  • Independence is the goal. Every support should have a plan to fade.
  • Ask "How will we reduce this?" from the very beginning.
  • Natural supports beat artificial ones. Build relationships, not dependence.

3. "Appropriate" Doesn't Mean "Optimal"

This is probably the most important legal concept every parent needs to understand, and it's one schools rarely explain clearly: Schools are required to provide a "Free Appropriate Public Education" (FAPE), not the best possible education.

Let me be blunt: "Appropriate" is a pretty low bar. It doesn't mean your child will reach their full potential. It doesn't mean they'll get every service you think they need. It means the school must provide an education that is "reasonably calculated to enable a child to make progress appropriate in light of the child's circumstances."

Notice what that doesn't say. It doesn't say "maximum progress." It doesn't say "optimal outcomes." It says "appropriate progress."

I watched a family spend $50,000 on due process fighting for 30 minutes of daily speech therapy instead of the 20 minutes the school offered. They had research showing more is better. They had private evaluations recommending it. They lost.

Why? Because the hearing officer found that with 20 minutes of speech therapy, their daughter was making "some progress." Not great progress. Not the progress she could have made with more. But some progress. And legally, that's appropriate.

This is hard to accept when it's your child. You want optimal, not appropriate. You want everything that could possibly help. I get it. But understanding this legal standard will save you heartache and help you fight the battles you can actually win.

Here's when you have a case:

  • When your child is making no progress or regressing
  • When the school isn't implementing what's in the IEP
  • When the school refuses to evaluate despite documented concerns
  • When supports are clearly inadequate for your child to access education
  • When the proposed placement can't meet your child's needs

Here's when you probably don't:

  • When you want more of a service that's already producing some progress
  • When you prefer a different methodology than what the school uses
  • When you want your child in a different classroom with a specific teacher
  • When you disagree with how much progress is "enough"
  • When you want private school placement because it's "better"

I know this sounds discouraging. But here's the thing: understanding the legal standard helps you focus your energy where it matters. Instead of fighting for incremental increases in minutes, focus on documenting whether your child is making progress. Keep work samples. Track IEP goals. Take notes on behaviors at home. Request progress reports.

If your child truly isn't making progress, you have legal grounds to request changes. But if they're making some progress, even if you think they could make more, that legal battle is uphill and expensive.

"Save your fight for when it really matters—when your child isn't making progress at all."

Key Takeaway:

  • Know the legal standard. Schools must provide appropriate, not optimal.
  • Progress is the key metric. Document everything related to progress.
  • Some battles aren't worth fighting. Save your energy for what matters.
  • Keep detailed records. Data is your most powerful tool.
  • Understand when you have leverage. No progress = you have a case.

4. Behavior Is Communication

If I could give parents just one lens to view their child through, it would be this: Every behavior is communication. Even—especially—the challenging ones.

When your child throws their work across the room, hits another student, refuses to transition, or melts down in the cafeteria, they're not trying to be difficult. They're communicating something they don't have the words or skills to express differently.

After three decades of doing functional behavior assessments, I can tell you that most challenging behaviors fall into a few categories:

  • "This is too hard" - The work is above their skill level, too long, or too frustrating
  • "I'm overwhelmed" - Too much sensory input, too many people, too much noise
  • "I want to escape" - This situation is unpleasant and I need out
  • "I need attention" - I don't know how to get positive attention, so negative will do
  • "I want something" - I see something I want and don't have words to ask

Jake was labeled as having "severe behavioral problems." Every day in math, he'd rip up his worksheet, throw pencils, and end up in the office. His teacher thought he was defiant. His parents were getting daily calls. Everyone focused on stopping the behavior.

I spent one day observing and the pattern was obvious: Jake could do single-digit addition just fine. But when the worksheet had double-digit problems, he'd work for about 90 seconds, struggle, then escalate. He wasn't defiant. He was communicating: "This is too hard and I don't know how to ask for help."

Once we taught him to raise his hand and say "I need help," and once we modified the work to his actual level, the "severe behavioral problems" disappeared completely. No behavior plan needed. Just understanding what he was trying to communicate.

This is where the ABC model becomes incredibly powerful:

A - Antecedent: What happens right before the behavior?
B - Behavior: What exactly does the child do?
C - Consequence: What happens right after?

When you start tracking this pattern, you often see the function of the behavior clearly. If your child acts out every time you ask them to write and then gets to skip writing, they've learned that acting out = escape from writing. The behavior works for them.

But here's what most people miss: Punishment won't fix this. You can't punish away communication. You can temporarily suppress a behavior, but if you haven't taught a replacement skill, the child will find another way to communicate the same message. Maybe instead of hitting, they'll elope. Maybe instead of screaming, they'll shut down completely.

The solution isn't to stop the behavior—it's to teach a better way to communicate the same message. If the child is trying to escape hard work, teach them to ask for a break. If they want attention, teach them appropriate ways to get it. If they're overwhelmed, teach them to request a quiet space.

"You can't punish away communication. You have to teach a better way to express the same need."

I've seen parents and teachers transform challenging situations by simply changing their lens. Instead of asking "How do I stop this behavior?" they ask "What is this behavior communicating?" and "What replacement skill can I teach?"

Key Takeaway:

  • Every behavior has a function. Figure out what need it's meeting.
  • Track the ABC pattern. Data reveals what's really happening.
  • Teach replacement skills. Give them a better way to communicate.
  • Punishment doesn't teach. It just suppresses without solving.
  • Ask "What are they trying to tell me?" not "How do I make this stop?"

5. You and the School Are on the Same Team

I know this might be hard to believe, especially if you've had difficult experiences. But after sitting on both sides of that IEP table for 35 years, I can tell you: Most educators genuinely want to help your child.

They're not your enemy. They're not trying to deny services to save money. They're not lazy or uncaring. They're human beings who chose a profession where they could make a difference, and most of them care deeply about the kids they serve.

But—and this is important—they're working within real constraints. Limited budgets. High caseloads. Insufficient training. Overwhelming paperwork. Legal requirements that sometimes conflict with what they know is best. They're often as frustrated by the system as you are.

I remember Sarah, a special education teacher with a caseload of 28 students, each with their own IEP goals to track. She was also expected to attend every IEP meeting, write progress reports quarterly, coordinate with general education teachers, and somehow actually teach. When parents came in angry and accusatory, I watched her shut down. Not because she didn't care, but because she was already drowning and felt attacked for circumstances beyond her control.

But when parents came in as partners—acknowledging the challenges, asking "How can we work together on this?"—I watched her move mountains. She'd stay after school to create materials. She'd text the parents with updates. She'd advocate within the system for what the child needed. The difference wasn't the child's needs; it was the relationship.

I've seen the same IEP meeting go two completely different ways based on the approach. When parents come in adversarial—with advocates, making demands, threatening due process—the school's walls go up. They get defensive. They lawyer up. They stop being creative and start being strictly compliant. Nobody wins, especially not the child.

But when parents come in collaborative—appreciating what's working, clearly articulating concerns with data, asking for partnership in problem-solving—magic happens. Teachers get creative. Principals find resources. Everyone brainstorms solutions.

This doesn't mean being a pushover. You can be collaborative while also being firm about your child's needs. You can disagree respectfully. You can hold the school accountable while maintaining a positive relationship. It's not either/or.

Here's what collaborative advocacy looks like:

  • Start by acknowledging what's working: "I really appreciate how Mrs. Johnson makes Alex feel welcome in her classroom."
  • Come with data, not just feelings: "I've been tracking his reading at home, and here's what I'm seeing..."
  • Ask questions instead of making accusations: "Can you help me understand why we're seeing such different behaviors at school versus home?"
  • Propose solutions, not just problems: "I'm wondering if we could try..."
  • Recognize constraints: "I know resources are limited. What if we..."

I'm not saying schools are always right. They're not. Sometimes they make mistakes. Sometimes you do need to push back firmly. Sometimes you need an advocate. But save the fight for when it really matters—when your child isn't making progress, when services aren't being implemented, when their needs truly aren't being met.

Don't fight over every accommodation, every minute of service, every perceived slight. You'll burn out, the school will tune you out, and your child will be caught in the middle.

"Partnership beats adversarial every single time. Save the fight for when it truly matters."

Building strong relationships with your child's teachers and service providers pays dividends. They'll go the extra mile. They'll text you with concerns before they become crises. They'll advocate for your child in team meetings you're not in. They'll actually implement the IEP with fidelity because they're invested, not just compliant.

One of my favorite parents ever used to bring coffee to IEP meetings—not expensive stuff, just decent coffee in a nice thermos. Such a small gesture, but it set a tone: We're in this together. Let's sit down and figure this out. It worked every time.

Key Takeaway:

  • Most educators genuinely care. Start with that assumption.
  • Acknowledge constraints. They're working within real limitations.
  • Partnership beats adversarial. Collaboration gets better results.
  • Save the fight. Don't battle over everything.
  • Build relationships. Invested teachers go the extra mile.

Moving Forward Together

These five lessons took me 35 years to learn—35 years of IEP meetings, classroom observations, parent conferences, and watching what actually helps children thrive versus what just sounds good on paper.

I wish I could tell you that special education is straightforward, that schools always get it right, that there's a perfect playbook to follow. But the truth is messier and more human than that. It requires you to be your child's biggest advocate while also being their primary teacher. It requires understanding legal realities while maintaining hope. It requires seeing behavior as communication and building partnerships even when it's hard.

Most of all, it requires you to embrace your power—not the power to fight the school, but the power to fundamentally shape your child's trajectory through what you do every single day at home, through the expectations you set, the consistency you maintain, and the skills you build.

You are not just a parent. You are your child's first teacher, their primary advocate, and their greatest influence. No IEP can replace what you bring to your child's life.
