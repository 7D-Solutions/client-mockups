The ABC Model: Understanding Why Your Child Does What They Do

September 25, 2024 • 11 min read

You've seen the behavior a hundred times. The meltdown at bedtime. The defiance when asked to do homework. The tantrums in the grocery store. And every time, you're left asking the same question: "Why does my child keep doing this?"

Here's the truth that changes everything: All behavior has a reason. Every single action your child takes—from the screaming to the refusing to the hitting—serves a purpose. It's not random. It's not just to drive you crazy. And once you understand the "why" behind the behavior, you gain the power to change it.

That's where the ABC Model comes in. This simple but powerful framework is used by behavior analysts, special educators, and therapists worldwide to decode even the most challenging behaviors. And now, it's in your toolkit.

What Is the ABC Model?

The ABC Model is a framework for understanding behavior by examining what happens before, during, and after a behavior occurs. It stands for Antecedent, Behavior, Consequence—three critical pieces of information that reveal why a behavior happens and what keeps it going.

A = Antecedent (The Trigger)

The antecedent is everything that happened right before the behavior. It's the trigger, the setting event, the thing that set the stage. Think of it as the "spark" that ignites the behavior.

Examples of antecedents:

  • Being told "no"
  • Transition from playtime to homework
  • Loud noises or bright lights
  • Seeing a preferred item (toy, snack, screen)
  • Being asked to wait
  • Another child taking a toy
  • Parent talking on the phone

B = Behavior (What Actually Happened)

This is the specific action you observed—described as objectively as possible. No interpretations, no assumptions about intent. Just the facts. Ask yourself: "What would a video camera have recorded?"

Good behavior descriptions:

  • ✅ "Hit the table with both fists three times"
  • ✅ "Threw iPad on the floor"
  • ✅ "Screamed 'I hate you!' and ran to bedroom"
  • ✅ "Fell to the floor, kicked legs, cried loudly for 5 minutes"

Poor behavior descriptions (too vague or interpretive):

  • ❌ "Had a tantrum" (what did that look like exactly?)
  • ❌ "Was being manipulative" (interpretation, not observation)
  • ❌ "Got upset" (what did upset look like?)
  • ❌ "Acted out" (too general)

C = Consequence (What Happened After)

The consequence is what happened immediately after the behavior. This includes how people responded, what the child got (or avoided), and any changes in the environment.

Examples of consequences:

  • Parent gave in and provided the requested item
  • Child was sent to time-out (removed from situation)
  • Teacher provided one-on-one attention
  • Task was removed or child was excused
  • Everyone in the room looked at the child
  • Nothing changed (behavior continued)
Key Insight: Consequences Drive Future Behavior

If a behavior "works"—meaning it gets the child something they want or helps them avoid something they don't want—that behavior will happen again. This is the fundamental principle of behavioral learning: behavior that is reinforced will continue.

The Four Functions of Behavior

Here's where it gets really powerful. After analyzing hundreds of thousands of behaviors, researchers have discovered that all behaviors can be categorized into just four functions. Every single behavior your child exhibits serves one of these four purposes:

1
ESCAPE/AVOIDANCE
"I want to get away from this"

The behavior happens to GET AWAY from something uncomfortable, difficult, or unwanted.

Common triggers:

  • Task is too hard or frustrating
  • Sensory overload (too loud, too bright, too much)
  • Uncomfortable social situation
  • Physical discomfort
  • Boring or repetitive activity

What to look for: Behavior increases when demands are placed and decreases when demands are removed.

2
ATTENTION
"Look at me, notice me"

The behavior happens to GET attention from others—even if that attention is negative.

Common scenarios:

  • Parent is busy with another child or task
  • Child has been alone or ignored
  • Doing things that get a big reaction
  • Repeating behaviors that make adults respond

What to look for: Behavior happens when attention is low and stops when attention is provided (even scolding counts as attention!).

3
ACCESS TO TANGIBLES
"I want that thing"

The behavior happens to GET something the child wants—a toy, food, activity, or privilege.

Common examples:

  • Wants a specific toy or item
  • Wants access to a preferred activity (screen time, playground)
  • Wants food or treats
  • Wants to keep playing when told to stop

What to look for: Behavior happens when the child is told "no" or when a preferred item is removed or denied.

4
SENSORY/AUTOMATIC
"This feels good"

The behavior happens because it FEELS GOOD or meets a sensory need—the reinforcement is internal, not from the environment.

Common examples:

  • Stimming (hand flapping, rocking, spinning)
  • Repetitive sounds or movements
  • Some forms of self-injury (provides sensory input)
  • Touching certain textures repeatedly

What to look for: Behavior happens regardless of what's going on around the child—in any environment, with or without attention.

How to Do an ABC Analysis

Now that you understand the components and functions, let's put it all together. Here's your step-by-step process for conducting an ABC analysis at home:

1
Observe and Record

When the behavior happens, immediately write down:

  • Date and time of the incident
  • Where it happened (location and who was present)
  • Antecedent: What was happening right before?
  • Behavior: Exactly what did the child do? (Be specific and objective)
  • Consequence: What happened immediately after?

Don't wait! Memory fades quickly. Record it as soon as possible.

2
Look for Patterns

After collecting data for at least 5-10 instances of the behavior, review your notes and ask:

  • When does this behavior USUALLY happen? (Time of day? Specific situations?)
  • What's the common trigger or antecedent?
  • What does the child consistently GET from this behavior?
  • What does the child consistently AVOID from this behavior?
3
Identify the Function

Based on the patterns you've identified, determine which function the behavior serves:

  • Escape? Does the behavior help the child get out of something?
  • Attention? Does the behavior result in attention from others?
  • Tangible? Does the behavior result in getting a preferred item or activity?
  • Sensory? Does the behavior seem to feel good regardless of what's happening around them?

Real-World Examples: The ABC Model in Action

Let's look at four different scenarios that illustrate each function of behavior:

Example 1: Hitting During Math (ESCAPE Function)
  • Antecedent: Teacher says "Time to start your math worksheets"
  • Behavior: Child hits the table with both fists and screams "No!"
  • Consequence: Child is sent to the calm-down corner for 10 minutes; misses math practice
  • Function: ESCAPE—the behavior successfully got the child out of doing math

What's happening: The child finds math difficult or frustrating. The behavior (hitting/screaming) is reinforced because it results in avoiding the difficult task. Even though it might look like punishment (being sent away), for this child, it's actually a reward—they escaped math!

Example 2: Screaming in the Store (TANGIBLE Function)
  • Antecedent: Child sees candy at the checkout, parent says "No, not today"
  • Behavior: Child screams loudly and drops to the floor, kicks legs, cries
  • Consequence: After 2 minutes, parent says "Fine, just this once" and gives the candy
  • Function: TANGIBLE—the behavior successfully got the desired item (candy)

What's happening: The child learned that screaming and dropping to the floor is an effective way to get candy. The behavior is strongly reinforced because it works—even if the parent only gives in "sometimes," intermittent reinforcement is actually the strongest kind.

Example 3: Making Noises in Class (ATTENTION Function)
  • Antecedent: Teacher is helping another student at the back of the room
  • Behavior: Child makes loud silly noises ("beep beep beep!")
  • Consequence: Teacher turns around and says "Stop that right now!"
  • Function: ATTENTION—the behavior successfully got the teacher to look and respond

What's happening: The child wanted the teacher's attention. Even though the teacher's response was corrective, it was still attention—and it worked! For a child seeking attention, negative attention is better than no attention.

Example 4: Hand Flapping (SENSORY Function)
  • Antecedent: No specific trigger—happens in various situations throughout the day
  • Behavior: Child flaps hands rapidly near face for 30 seconds
  • Consequence: Nothing specific—child continues with activity, behavior doesn't change based on response
  • Function: SENSORY—the behavior feels good and/or provides sensory regulation

What's happening: This behavior serves an internal sensory need. It's not about getting something from the environment or avoiding anything—it just feels good or helps the child regulate. You'll notice sensory behaviors happen regardless of who's around or what's happening.

What to Do With This Information

Understanding the function is only the first step. The real power comes from using this information to create effective interventions. Here's how to respond based on each function:

For ESCAPE Behaviors

Goal: Teach appropriate ways to ask for breaks while building tolerance for difficult tasks.

  • Don't let the behavior = escape. If hitting gets the child out of math, hitting will continue. Stay calm and neutral, then redirect back to the task.
  • Teach appropriate requesting. "If you need a break, you can say 'break please' or tap this card." Practice this during calm times.
  • Modify the task initially. If math is too hard, start with easier problems to build success and gradually increase difficulty.
  • Build tolerance gradually. Start with 2 minutes of work, then break. Gradually increase work time.
  • Provide choices within the task. "Do you want to do the even problems or odd problems first?"

For ATTENTION Behaviors

Goal: Provide abundant attention for positive behaviors and minimize attention for problem behaviors.

  • Give attention proactively. Don't wait for the behavior. Provide frequent positive attention throughout the day.
  • Teach appropriate attention-seeking. "If you need me, tap my arm and say 'Mom' or 'excuse me.'"
  • Respond to appropriate requests immediately. When they ask appropriately, stop what you're doing and respond—this reinforces the replacement behavior.
  • Minimize attention for problem behaviors. Stay calm, neutral facial expression, minimal words. Then redirect to appropriate behavior.
  • Never give in. If you provide the attention they're seeking through problem behavior, you've just taught them it works.

For TANGIBLE Behaviors

Goal: Teach appropriate requesting and maintain consistent boundaries.

  • Never give the item when problem behavior happens. If screaming gets candy, screaming will continue. Stay firm—no matter how intense it gets.
  • Teach appropriate requesting. "If you want something, you can ask nicely: 'May I please have...'"
  • Practice during calm times. Role-play asking for things and getting them when asked appropriately.
  • Offer choices within acceptable options. "You can have carrots or crackers" (not candy, which isn't an option).
  • Stay absolutely consistent. "No" must mean no, every time. Giving in even once makes the behavior stronger (intermittent reinforcement).

For SENSORY Behaviors

Goal: Provide acceptable alternatives and scheduled sensory input.

  • Understand you may not eliminate it. If a behavior serves a sensory need, trying to stop it completely may not be realistic or helpful.
  • Provide acceptable alternatives. If hand-flapping is disruptive in class, teach them to squeeze a stress ball or use a fidget instead.
  • Schedule sensory breaks. Provide regular opportunities for sensory input throughout the day.
  • Consider occupational therapy. An OT can help identify sensory needs and develop a "sensory diet."
  • Redirect when necessary. If a sensory behavior is harmful or highly disruptive, gently redirect to an acceptable alternative: "I see you need to move. Let's do jumping jacks."

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Guessing Without Data

Don't assume you know the function based on how the behavior "looks" or what you think the child's intent is. "He does it to annoy me" is almost never the actual function. Collect ABC data before deciding on the function.

2. Missing the Real Function

Remember: The same behavior can serve different functions in different contexts. Screaming might be for attention at home but for escape at school. Always analyze the specific ABCs of each situation.

3. Accidentally Reinforcing the Behavior

This is the big one. If your consequence gives the child what they want (attention, escape, tangible) or helps them avoid what they don't want, you've just reinforced the behavior—which means it will happen again.

Ask yourself: "What did this behavior GET my child or help them AVOID?" If the answer is something they wanted, the behavior was reinforced.

4. Focusing Only on Stopping the Behavior

Behavior serves a purpose. If you only focus on stopping it without teaching what to do instead, you haven't solved the problem—you've just created a vacuum. The child still has the need; they just don't have an appropriate way to meet it.

Teaching Replacement Behaviors: The Key to Lasting Change

Here's the principle that ties everything together: Don't just stop the behavior—teach what to do instead.

Every problem behavior is meeting a need. If you eliminate the behavior without providing an appropriate alternative, you haven't solved anything. The child still has the need; now they just don't have a way to meet it. That's when you see behavior escalate or new problem behaviors emerge.

Criteria for an Effective Replacement Behavior

A good replacement behavior must meet three criteria:

  1. Same function: It must serve the same purpose as the problem behavior
  2. Easier to do: It must require less effort than the problem behavior
  3. Works quickly: It must get the desired result at least as fast (preferably faster)

Replacement Behavior Examples

  • Instead of hitting to escape a hard task → Teach "I need a break" or break card
  • Instead of screaming for candy → Teach "May I please have..." (and sometimes say yes when asked nicely!)
  • Instead of making noises for attention → Teach "Excuse me" or hand-raising
  • Instead of disruptive stimming → Teach to use a fidget tool or request a sensory break

Critical point: You must respond to the replacement behavior immediately and consistently. If you teach "say break please" but then ignore it or say "not now," the child will go back to hitting—because hitting worked and the replacement didn't.

The Bottom Line

The ABC Model isn't just about analyzing behavior—it's about understanding your child, meeting their needs appropriately, and building a foundation of effective communication and self-regulation that will serve them for life.

When you understand the "why," the "how to change it" becomes clear. That's the power of the ABC Model.
