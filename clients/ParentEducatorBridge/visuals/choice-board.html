Choice Board System

Visual Choice Tools for Children with Autism & Special Needs

Why Choice Boards Reduce Problem Behavior

Choice boards are powerful tools that give children appropriate control while reducing anxiety and frustration. When children can communicate their preferences visually, they are less likely to engage in challenging behaviors.

Benefits of Choice Boards:

  • Reduces Anxiety: Children know what options are available and don't have to guess
  • Appropriate Control: Provides choices within adult-determined boundaries
  • Prevents Overwhelm: Limits options to a manageable number
  • Supports Communication: Visual support for children with limited verbal skills
  • Builds Decision-Making: Teaches children to make choices independently
  • Reduces Power Struggles: Child feels empowered while adult maintains structure

How to Introduce Choice Boards:

  1. Start Small: Begin with 2 highly preferred items
  2. Model First: Show your child how to point or touch their choice
  3. Honor Choices: Always follow through with the choice they make
  4. Use Consistently: Incorporate into daily routines
  5. Expand Gradually: Add more options as child becomes comfortable
Key Principle: The power of choice boards comes from giving children control over things that don't really matter (which snack, which toy) so they accept your control over things that do matter (safety, routines, boundaries).

Tips for Offering Appropriate Choices:

  • Limited Options: 2-6 choices maximum to prevent overwhelm
  • All Acceptable: Only offer choices you're willing to honor
  • Equal Value: All options should be similarly desirable/acceptable
  • Timed Boundaries: "You can choose now, or I'll choose for you in 10 seconds"
  • Visual Support: Use pictures/icons for non-readers or visual learners
  • Consistent Format: Keep the board layout consistent for predictability

When NOT to Offer Choices:

  • Safety Issues: "You must hold my hand in the parking lot" (non-negotiable)
  • Health Requirements: "You need to take your medicine" (not optional)
  • Time Constraints: "We're leaving in 2 minutes" (no time for prolonged choosing)
  • After Problem Behavior: Don't offer choices immediately after tantrums (this reinforces the behavior)
  • When You're Not Sure: Only offer choices when you're ready to honor them
Remember: If you wouldn't be okay with either option, don't make it a choice. Say "It's time to brush teeth" instead of "Do you want to brush your teeth?"

How to Print and Use:

  • Print on cardstock for durability
  • Laminate for repeated use
  • Cut out individual cards for flexible arrangements
  • Use velcro dots to create reusable boards
  • Keep a marker or token for child to place on their choice
  • Store completed boards in page protectors or binders
