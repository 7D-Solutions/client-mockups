Using This Chart Effectively

This expected vs. unexpected behavior chart is designed to help children with autism and special needs develop social awareness and self-regulation skills. The focus is on teaching replacement behaviors rather than simply correcting problem behaviors.

Teaching Expected Behaviors (Not Shaming)

  • Use positive language: Focus on "Let's practice using our quiet voice" rather than "Stop yelling!"
  • Model the behavior: Demonstrate the expected behavior and explain how it makes others feel
  • Practice during calm times: Role-play scenarios when the child is regulated, not during meltdowns
  • Celebrate small wins: Notice and praise when the child uses expected behaviors
  • Avoid shame or embarrassment: Frame unexpected behaviors as learning opportunities, not failures

Proactive vs. Reactive Use

Proactive (Recommended):

  • Review the chart before transitions (e.g., "Let's look at our lunchroom expectations")
  • Practice expected behaviors through role-play and social stories
  • Use visual reminders posted in relevant areas (classroom, cafeteria, etc.)
  • Prime the child before challenging situations
  • Create a routine of reviewing expectations daily

Reactive (Use Sparingly):

  • After an unexpected behavior, calmly refer to the chart: "Let's see what we can do instead"
  • Help the child identify the replacement behavior
  • Practice the expected behavior immediately if possible
  • Avoid using the chart as punishment or shame

Role-Playing Scenarios

How to Practice:

  • Set up the scenario: "Let's pretend we're in the lunchroom and you want to sit with your friend"
  • Act it out: Use toys, stuffed animals, or take turns playing different roles
  • Practice the expected behavior: Walk through the steps together
  • Show the feelings: Use the feeling icons to show how others react to expected vs. unexpected behaviors
  • Make it fun: Use humor, exaggeration, and positive reinforcement
  • Repeat regularly: Practice the same scenarios multiple times until they become routine

Adapting for Individual Needs

  • Verbal children: Use the "People think I'm ___ when I ___" format to build perspective-taking
  • Nonverbal children: Focus on visual icons and feelings faces, use gesture prompts
  • High support needs: Start with 2-3 core behaviors, use simplified language and larger visuals
  • Lower support needs: Add more nuanced scenarios and discuss "why" behind expectations

Creating Consistency Across Environments

  • Use the same chart at home and school for consistency
  • Share with all adults who work with the child (teachers, therapists, family members)
  • Use consistent language when referring to behaviors
  • Post visual reminders in relevant locations
  • Review expectations before new or challenging situations

Important Reminders

  • This is not about compliance: The goal is helping the child understand social expectations and make informed choices
  • Progress takes time: Be patient and celebrate small improvements
  • Context matters: What's expected in one setting may not apply to another
  • Behavior is communication: Unexpected behaviors often signal unmet needs (sensory, communication, regulation)
  • Focus on teaching, not punishing: Every unexpected behavior is an opportunity to teach the replacement

Additional Resources

Consider pairing this chart with:

  • Visual schedules to reduce anxiety and increase predictability
  • Emotion regulation tools (feelings thermometer, calm-down strategies)
  • Social stories for specific situations
  • Reinforcement systems (token boards, choice boards)
  • Sensory breaks and movement opportunities
Remember: The most effective behavior support comes from understanding the "why" behind the behavior and teaching the child alternative ways to meet their needs. This chart is a tool for teaching, not a punishment system.
