Where Do You Feel It In Your Body?

Head & Face

  • Happy: Smiling mouth, bright eyes
  • Sad: Heavy head, tears, droopy face
  • Angry: Hot face, tight jaw, frowning
  • Worried: Furrowed brow, biting lip
  • Confused: Tilted head, squinted eyes

Chest & Tummy

  • Scared: Fast heartbeat, tight chest
  • Excited: Butterflies, fluttery feeling
  • Nervous: Upset tummy, knots
  • Calm: Slow breathing, relaxed
  • Hungry: Empty tummy, rumbling

Arms & Hands

  • Angry: Tight fists, tense muscles
  • Frustrated: Clenched hands, stiff arms
  • Calm: Loose hands, relaxed arms
  • Excited: Wiggly fingers, moving hands

Whole Body

  • Tired: Heavy body, want to lay down
  • Sick: Achy, weak, no energy
  • Energetic: Want to move, jump, run
  • Calm: Still, peaceful, comfortable

Parent & Educator Guide: Using the Feelings Chart

How to Teach Emotion Identification

  • Start simple: Begin with 3-4 basic emotions (happy, sad, angry, calm) before introducing more complex feelings
  • Model emotions: Make faces and label your own emotions throughout the day: "I feel happy when we play together!"
  • Use visual supports: Point to the chart when your child experiences an emotion in real-time
  • Practice during calm moments: Review the chart when your child is regulated, not during meltdowns
  • Connect to body sensations: Help children identify where they feel emotions physically (butterflies in tummy = nervous)
  • Read emotion books together: Reinforce learning through stories about feelings
  • Play emotion games: Use the cut-out cards for matching, sorting, or acting out emotions
Tip for Nonverbal Children: Use the check-in strip for quick communication. Laminate it and attach to a keyring or keep it accessible for your child to point to their current feeling.

When to Use the Chart

  • Morning check-ins: "How do you feel this morning?" to start the day with emotional awareness
  • After transitions: School pick-up, bedtime, or activity changes when emotions may shift
  • During teachable moments: When you notice your child expressing an emotion
  • Before challenging activities: "How are you feeling about going to the store?"
  • As a regulation tool: When you notice early signs of dysregulation
  • Bedtime reflection: "What feelings did you have today?"
  • NOT during meltdowns: Wait until your child is calm and receptive
Zones of Regulation Integration: The emotions are color-coded to align with Zones of Regulation. Use this to help your child understand their energy level and self-regulation needs. Blue = low energy, Green = regulated, Yellow = elevated energy, Red = extremely high energy/dysregulated.

How to Validate Feelings

  • Acknowledge first: "I see you're feeling angry" before problem-solving
  • Normalize emotions: "It's okay to feel sad. Everyone feels sad sometimes."
  • Avoid dismissing: Don't say "You're fine" or "Don't cry" - this invalidates their experience
  • Name the emotion: "That frustrated feeling makes sense because your tower fell down"
  • Connect to body: "I notice your hands are in fists. Your body is showing you're frustrated."
  • Offer support: "What can help you feel better?" or "Would a hug help?"
  • Teach coping skills: "When I feel worried, I take deep breaths. Want to try together?"

Building Emotional Intelligence Over Time

  • Expand vocabulary: Once basic emotions are mastered, introduce related feelings (frustrated, disappointed, proud, content)
  • Discuss intensity: "Are you a little bit sad or very very sad?"
  • Teach multiple emotions: "You can feel excited AND nervous at the same time"
  • Encourage expression: Celebrate when your child identifies and communicates their feelings
  • Model your own emotions: "Mommy feels frustrated when I can't find my keys, so I'm taking deep breaths"
  • Connect emotions to choices: "I can see you're calm now. That's a great time to make good choices."

Special Considerations for Autism/Special Needs

  • Alexithymia awareness: Some autistic individuals have difficulty identifying their own emotions. Be patient and provide extra support.
  • Interoception challenges: Your child may struggle to recognize internal body signals. Explicitly teach body-emotion connections.
  • Visual processing strengths: Leverage the visual nature of the chart - many autistic children are strong visual learners
  • Routine integration: Make emotion check-ins a predictable part of the daily schedule
  • Reduce verbal demands: Allow pointing, using AAC device, or other non-verbal communication methods
  • Sensory considerations: Some emotions may be primarily sensory (overwhelmed, understimulated) - add these if relevant
  • Concrete examples: Use specific scenarios: "Remember when your toy broke? That's when you felt frustrated."
Remember: Emotional regulation is a skill that develops over time. Your child is learning to understand and manage their internal world - this is complex work! Celebrate small victories and be patient with the process. You're doing a wonderful job supporting your child's emotional development.

Feelings Chart Accessibility Information

This feelings chart is designed to help children identify and communicate their emotions. Each emotion is represented by a large emoji-style face with a color-coded border and label. The chart includes 12 core emotions: Happy (yellow), Sad (blue), Angry (red), Scared (purple), Worried (orange), Frustrated (dark red), Tired (gray), Sick (green), Excited (bright orange), Calm (teal), Confused (light gray), and Hungry (dark orange).
